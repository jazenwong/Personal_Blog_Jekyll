"[The] spirit of wabi sabi... may be summed up with the following three principles:

1. Nothing is perfect.
2. Nothing is finished.
3. Nothing lasts forever."

Wabi sabi encourages us to appreciate the present and the imperfect (as nothing can be perfect). In Japan, many of the most valued art pieces are ones that are damaged and restored (e.g. [kintsugi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kintsugi?useskin=vector)) or would be considered imperfect by Western standards, such as Honami Koetusu's "Mount Fuji."

![Mount Fuji by Honami Koetsu](/assets/blogimages/wabisabi/Fujisan.jpg#center#background "Mount Fuji")

Wabi sabi is known for its philisophical influences on not just aesthetics, but also living, which can translate to our work. Here are some wabi sabi principles for the workplace:

1. Appreciate the people you work with. You're not going to see them forever. Treasure the interactions that joyfully break the professional barrier, as these moments are rate.
2. Don't just chase one goal after another, whether it be for your own career or the company. Give yourself time to process what's happening.
3. Be kind to yourself when you make mistakes.
4. Learn when it's the right time to finish. What's shipped is better than what isn't.
5. When you're in a meeting, be present.
6. Your best work isn't behind you, you're always beginning.
7. You can't always be "up," there will inevitably be downs. But down periods aren't bad, they're an opportunity to appreciate and reflect.
8. The traits that are valuable to a team aren't exclusive to their performance. There are many other traits that one can have which have great value but are not easily measurable, such as team comradery.
9. By accepting the fact that our existence is fragile, we're prepared for the inevitable and can thus adopt an anti fragile state of mind.
10. Repairing a damaged relationship can make it better than it ever was.
